First draft of personal profile

Right, where do I start?

I knew from the start that I needed to create a website that stands out to potential employers. I read Kyle Cassidy's blog post on the importance of homepage design and he spoke about the "Halo effect". "The halo effect is a psychological phenomenon where a potential employer is more likely to be impressed by the candidate based solely on the initial impression." For this reason, I wanted to create the best first impression possible and show off my skills. So...

Chanelling the Halo effect

Welcome to the joy of the powers of CSS3 and the use of the "background-image: (url)" property. I had an idea in my head of a photo I wanted to take for the homepage, so I used a Canon 550d, DSLR with nice lighting and then got to work with photoshop. Firstly, changing the levels of the photo and cropping down to a suitable size. Then applied a sketch filter and there you have it (see figure 1).

So, great news, I was happy with how the edit had turned out, but even still I felt like there was work to be done. This is where I applied the background-blend technique to my CSS. I applied the "multiply" effect before finally coming to a final decision on which colour was most suitable (dark blue).

From this moment onwards, I had an idea about the colour palette for my whole site, and then kept these colours consistent throughout my site in order to generate a recognisable, clean house style from start to finish.

I knew this was important after reading Janet Odgis's post on the well established website Huffington Post on keeping colour consistent as she says "Colour is a way to differentiate yourself in a crowded marketplace".

After all, the amount of competition for jobs these days is challenging enough as it is. Therefore, standing out from the crowd and using bright, vibrant colours and a recognisable colour palette will enable users to easily identify my site compared to others.

Research

Considering this was my own personal site, the research phase was much less challenging than other projects I have worked on. I had the freedom to go in any direction I wanted in terms of design and build.

In order to get my brain working, I searched for some inspiration and found a few brilliant sites which I found really suited me and my style. I compiled my findings into a mood board (See figure 2 below). You'll notice that a few design features of these sites have had an impact on my own design.

Design

After I had completed researching, I moved to designing my site before even touching my laptop. I did this by sketching out multiple wireframes for mobile, tablet and desktop. Simply providing a basis for ideas and different designs can help greatly when laying out the HTML structure of the site.

I referred back to the colour palette I picked up on in my mood board (figure 2) but after trying out the colours, I realised they needed slightly tweaking for accessibility reasons. I was able to check whether certain colours were hard to see thanks to the use of a google chrome extension, Accessibility Developer Tools which would provide a rating on whether certain colours of text were too hard to read.

Build

When I started to get coding, I firstly provided a solid HTML5 structure before getting to work on the CSS or scripting. When all of my pages had a semantic structure in place I started to move on to the CSS.

The biggest challenges I face when coming to the build of any site is the positioning of elements, and flex-box can be such a pain sometimes! Thankfully, after a hard-fought battle with media queries and responsiveness, I got there in the end.

It wasn't over until I ran the site through an HTML validator and made sure my code was clean and concise while making sure shorthand CSS was used where possible in order to increase web-page performance.
